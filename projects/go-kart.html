Custom Go-Kart

Build video — add MP4 or YouTube embed when ready

Designed and built a single-seat, engine-driven go-kart from scratch over ~3 months on a student budget—covering concept, CAD, fabrication, wiring, and bring-up. Powered by a Lifan 212 cc electric-start with a custom push-to-start switch, driving a 30-series torque converter into a live axle on a 1×1×.120″ A513 steel chassis. Performance targets reflect the chosen non-shifter 4-stroke class (design goal ≈ 60 mph).

System Breakdown

Chassis — Design

Modeled in SolidWorks. We pivoted from a round-tube concept to square-tube A513 (1×1×.120″) to eliminate tube bending, keep cuts repeatable, and make jigging/alignment straightforward on a first build. Members were updated to accurate stock profiles; weldment cut list and drawings captured lengths and miters.

Chassis — Manufacturing

Fabricated in-house: cutting steel to length, cleaning/beveling with an angle grinder, tack-up, and weld-out. A planned weld sequence helps control distortion. (Welding/assembly ongoing.)

Powertrain / Drivetrain

Final choice: Lifan 212 cc Electric-Start (Honda GX200-style) → 30-series torque converter (Comet TAV2-class) → chain → live axle. The live axle uses hubs and lock collars to locate the sprocket, brake, and bearings so alignment stays fixed in use. We chose a CVT + live axle for reliability, simplicity, and cost over a gearbox/shifter setup.

Steering

Original steering shaft position placed the wheel too close. We swapped to a new shaft and planned a U-bend front crossbar as the column mount to improve ergonomics and alignment with the spindles. Final geometry targets comfortable reach and smooth bearings.

Braking System

Brake mount is part of the rear axle assembly and will be welded to the chassis. Layout leaves space for rotor/caliper alignment and service.

Electrical System

Custom push-to-start integrated with the Lifan harness: battery → main fuse → master → fused push-button branch → starter relay → starter. The kill switch grounds ignition. Grounds terminate at a single chassis point.

Engineering Process

Concept & Research

We started by exploring a shifter kart, but typical performance (e.g., 0–60 ≈ 4 s) and safety/tolerance demands were beyond first-build scope. We pivoted to a non-shifter 4-stroke single-seater to focus on fundamentals, reliability, and safety—while keeping a fast, fun target (~60 mph).

Engine Selection

Predator 212 was attractive (proven 6.5 HP, aftermarket, 3/4″ keyed shaft to TAV2), but electric-start conversion would require a ring-gear flywheel, starter, battery system, shroud mods, and possibly crank machining—adding cost/time/risk. We chose the Lifan 212 cc ES because it ships with electric-start and a harness that supports a clean push-button start, with similar power, GX200-style compatibility, and the same 3/4″ keyed interface.

Materials & BOM

Final chassis stock: A513 1×1×.120″ square tube. Ordered 60 ft (6×10 ft bars) against a ~477 in (~39.8 ft) requirement to cover kerf, angled cuts, and mistakes. Weldment profiles were initially missing/locked in Program Files; fixed by installing profiles in a user folder and re-linking SolidWorks, so members reflect real stock.

Bring-Up & Validation

  • Engine tests: first ignition via recoil; electric-start validated. Break-in plan: conventional 10W-30 for 3–5 hrs, then full synthetic.
  • Electrical: push-to-start circuit built/tested; correct fusing; retains factory key-start.
  • Structural (FEA): static checks run on steering crossbar/column area, front rails, seat supports, and rear rails—deflection/stress acceptable for a 180 lb driver.
  • Drivetrain issue: TAV backplate restricted belt alignment vs. engine-slot travel → Fix: spacers + slotted secondary bracket to set center distance and keep TAV parallel.
  • Electrical issue: starter latched once due to shared return with kill switch → Fix: separate returns + dedicated ground strap to starter case; ferrule-crimped terminations.

Skills & Takeaways

  • CAD design: SolidWorks weldments, member drawings, configurable stock profiles.
  • Fabrication: measuring, cutting, grinding/beveling, tack/weld sequence planning.
  • Systems integration: engine + CVT + live axle layout; steering and brake layout; lock-collar/hub positioning.
  • Electrical integration: push-to-start circuit design with correct fusing/relay logic; grounding strategy.
  • Structural checks: quick FEA to sanity-check deflection/stress on high-load features.
  • Project management: cost vs. capability trade-offs, BOM/stock planning (60 ft vs. ~477 in required), and iterative fixes.
