Effects of Varying Porous Structures of an Airfoil

USC Viterbi SHINE — porous-hole patterns on a NACA 0012 studied with SolidWorks CAD, Ansys simulations, and water-channel experiments.

Project Overview

As part of the USC Viterbi SHINE (Summer High School Intensive in Next-Generation Engineering) program, I conducted a research project exploring how porous airfoil structures affect lift and drag.

  • Investigate whether introducing porous holes could reduce drag and increase lift.
  • Improve overall aerodynamic efficiency for future aircraft designs.
  • Produce non-dimensional results that scale to larger aerospace applications.
Objectives & Design Parameters
  • Primary objective: reduce drag and increase lift to improve efficiency.
  • Porous holes: varied spacing and size.
  • Spacing: 10%, 15%, 20% of chord length.
  • Hole diameters: adjusted across configurations for comparison.
  • Angles of attack tested: 3°, 6°, 9°, 12°.
  • Airfoil model: NACA 0012.
  • Reynolds number: fixed at 5 × 104 for consistency with prior work.

Formulas used. Lift coefficient CL and drag coefficient CD were computed using standard non-dimensional relationships so results generalize beyond the test scale.

Methods & Tools

Simulations (Ansys + MATLAB). Modeled airflow around porous and non-porous airfoils; built meshes with appropriate boundary conditions; simulated velocity profiles, pressure contours, and lift/drag coefficients at multiple angles of attack.

CAD & Experiments (SolidWorks + 3D Printing). Designed porous airfoils in SolidWorks; 3D-printed in two halves, then finished and assembled. Conducted physical tests in a water channel, using dye for separation visualization and a high-speed camera for velocity capture.

Results
  • Flow behavior: porous holes reduced flow separation in some cases, delaying stall.
  • Lift/drag trends: at low AoA, porous configurations showed slightly reduced drag; at higher AoA, effects varied—some layouts improved the lift-to-drag ratio while others introduced additional turbulence.
  • Velocity profiles: water-channel measurements aligned with simulations, showing smoother downstream flow with optimized spacing.
